Know Your Venue

Versailles & the Gallery of Battles

Tonight you dine in the Galerie des Batailles — the largest room in the entire Chateau, a 120-metre corridor of French military glory inaugurated in 1837.

🏫

A Room of Epic Proportions

At 120 m long and 13 m wide, the Gallery rivals the Grande Galerie of the Louvre. Designed by architect Frederic Nepveu, it features marble columns, gilded stuccos, and glass ceilings flooding the space with light.

Architecture
🖼

33 Monumental Paintings

The walls display 33 massive canvases covering 1,300 years of French battles — from Clovis at Tolbiac (496) to Napoleon at Wagram (1809). Artists include Delacroix and Horace Vernet.

Art
⚔️

A Pantheon of Heroes

Beyond the paintings, 80 busts of officers killed in battle line the gallery, alongside bronze plaques bearing the names of princes, marshals, and admirals who fought for France.

Heritage
👑

Louis-Philippe's Masterpiece

By including victories from every dynasty — Merovingians through Bourbons, plus the Revolution and Napoleon — Louis-Philippe united a divided France under shared military glory.

History
🏢

What Stood Here Before

Before the gallery, these rooms were private apartments of Philippe I, Duke of Orleans (Louis XIV's brother) and his wife Elizabeth Charlotte of the Palatinate.

Anecdote
🎨

Delacroix's Battle of Taillebourg

The undisputed masterpiece: Delacroix's dramatic depiction of Louis IX charging across the bridge at Taillebourg in 1242 — one of the finest battle paintings ever created.

Masterpiece

From a humble hunting lodge to the grandest palace in Europe — Versailles is 400 years of ambition, art, and political theater condensed into 2,300 rooms.

🌳

From Swamp to Splendor

In 1623, Louis XIII built a simple hunting lodge on marshy woodland his courtiers mocked. His son Louis XIV transformed it into the most magnificent palace in Europe, moving his entire court here in 1682.

Origins
💎

The Hall of Mirrors

357 mirrors line the 73-metre gallery. France lured Venetian mirror-makers (risking death penalties from Venice) to break Italy's monopoly. When lit by 20,000 candles, it became a corridor of infinite light.

Icon
🌸

Gardens of Excess

The gardens span 800 hectares with 372 statues, 600 fountains, and 20 miles of water pipes. The Trianon flowers were once so intensely fragrant they made guests physically ill.

Gardens
📜

A Treaty That Changed the World

On June 28, 1919, the Treaty of Versailles was signed in the Hall of Mirrors, formally ending World War I — in the very room designed to celebrate French dominance over Europe.

World History
🐺

Europe's First Modern Zoo

Versailles housed an elaborate menagerie with exotic animals in purpose-built enclosures — a revolutionary concept that became the model for modern zoological gardens across Europe.

Anecdote
⛷️

The Royal Ice Rink

In winter, the Grand Canal froze over and became a spectacular ice-skating rink for the court — imagine the royals gliding across 1.6 km of ice framed by Le Notre's geometric gardens.

Fun Fact

Quick-fire facts to spark memorable conversations between courses. Each card is a 60-second read.

💡

"Did you know mirrors were once more valuable than gold?"

When Louis XIV lined an entire hall with 357 mirrors, he was making the 17th-century equivalent of a billionaire wrapping a building in diamonds. Venice had a total monopoly, and the craftsmen who left risked execution.

Icebreaker
🐱

"Louis XV brought his cat to cabinet meetings"

While Louis XIV choreographed elaborate court rituals around every daily act, his great-grandson Louis XV preferred the company of his cat during matters of state.

Icebreaker
🌎

"Native American diplomats once walked these halls"

In 1725, four Native American chiefs and a woman from the Mississippi Valley were received at Versailles on a diplomatic mission, meeting Louis XV in these very rooms.

Icebreaker
💐

"The flowers here once made people faint"

The Grand Trianon gardens were planted with such an intensity of fragrant blooms that guests often felt overwhelmed and had to retreat indoors.

Icebreaker
🔭

"This gallery was a blockchain before blockchain"

Louis-Philippe's Gallery of Battles was essentially an immutable public ledger of French military history — a permanent, chronological, verifiable record designed to be trusted by all. Sound familiar?

Web3 x History
🎫

"5,000 people lived here simultaneously"

At its peak, Versailles housed the entire French government, thousands of courtiers, and all their servants. The palace has 2,300 rooms and 67 staircases — the original co-working space.

Icebreaker
💰

"Versailles cost ~$300 billion in today's money"

Adjusted for inflation, the construction of Versailles is estimated to have cost hundreds of billions — a capital allocation decision that would make any VC pause.

Icebreaker
⚡

"The paintings around us span 1,300 years"

From Clovis's victory at Tolbiac in 496 AD to Napoleon at Wagram in 1809 — the 33 battle scenes on these walls compress over a millennium into a single room.
